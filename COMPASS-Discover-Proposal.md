## The Problem We Are Solving

Universities design degrees for a labour market that existed three to five years ago. By the time a curriculum goes through approval cycles, accreditation reviews, and academic governance, the employment landscape it was designed for has shifted. The gap between what a degree signals and what an employer actually needs has always been present. Generative AI has made that gap dangerous.

The specific problem is not that degrees are academically weak. Many are excellent. The problem is that **no university has a fast, evidence-based, repeatable way to answer the question that matters most to students, parents, employers, and institutional leaders: will this degree still be economically valuable in five years?**

Without a way to answer that question rigorously, curriculum review is anecdotal. Committees discuss reputation and peer benchmarks. Students choose based on brand. Employers absorb the cost of graduates who arrive underprepared for an AI-augmented workplace. And institutions only discover misalignment at graduation, not during design.

The COMPASS digital tool exists to close that gap.

---

## What COMPASS Is

The **Curriculum Outcomes and Market Proof Alignment Scoring System (COMPASS)** is a digital assessment tool built on top of generative AI, with a structured quality-assurance framework that promotes consistency, traceability, and evidence quality in its outputs. It evaluates whether a university degree, diploma, certificate, or professional training program will retain its economic value proposition in an AI-augmented labour market by 2027 and beyond.

It is not a course quality tool. It does not measure academic rigour, teaching quality, or student satisfaction. Those instruments already exist. COMPASS measures something different and currently unmeasured: **labour-market durability in an era of rapid automation**.

It delivers three integrated capabilities through a web-based interface:

1. **Viability Assessment** — Analyses any program using its publicly available curriculum handbook as the source of truth, producing a structured risk score and evidence-backed rationale across 11 dimensions.
2. **Market Intelligence** — Generates a real-time scan of relevant job families, recent hiring signals, and current professional discourse, anchoring the assessment in live labour-market evidence.
3. **Curriculum Redesign Advisor** — Converts the assessment and market evidence into a prioritised, implementation-ready curriculum improvement plan with interventions, owners, timelines, and measurable KPIs.

The full workflow produces an assessment in minutes that would take a curriculum advisory committee weeks to approximate — and it does so using evidence, not opinion.

## How It Works

### The COMPASS Scoring Rubric

COMPASS evaluates programs across **10 dimensions plus a bonus dimension**, each scored 0–3. The rubric is grounded in labour-market research including World Economic Forum Future of Jobs data, McKinsey generative AI automation analysis, and ILO AI and labour-market studies.

| # | Dimension | Why It Matters for 2027 |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Automation Exposure of Roles | Maps first 5 years of graduate work to current AI automation capability |
| 2 | Systems Thinking and Problem Framing | Differentiates graduates who design solutions from those who execute templates |
| 3 | Technical and Quantitative Depth | Hybrid technical-domain profiles are increasingly preferred in hiring |
| 4 | Decision-Making Under Uncertainty | Accountable judgment under constraints is where human value remains |
| 5 | AI Literacy and Governance | Required capability in virtually every knowledge-work role by 2027 |
| 6 | Domain Depth and Specialisation | Deep expertise is harder to replicate than general skill |
| 7 | Research Methods Rigor | Primary evidence generation and method defence signals durable capability |
| 8 | Human and Relational Capability | Clinical, care, and stakeholder-accountability skills remain strongly human |
| 9 | Curriculum Currency and Adaptability | Living curricula with advisory feedback loops stay aligned to market |
| 10 | Graduate Outcome Evidence | Granular role/salary/time-to-employment data enables evidence-based design |
| B | Irreplaceability Premium | Rare integration of technical depth, domain expertise, and human judgment |

### Risk Bands

| Score | Band | Interpretation |
|---|---|---|
| 28–36 | RESILIENT | AI augments rather than displaces graduates |
| 20–27 | MODERATE RISK | Pivot required; clear path to resilience available |
| 12–19 | HIGH RISK | Value proposition eroding; structural change needed |
| 0–11 | CRITICAL | Human middleware; graduates face rapid displacement |

### The Market Intelligence Capability

Beyond curriculum scoring, COMPASS integrates current labour-market evidence through its Market Intelligence capability. For each relevant job family, the assessment includes:
- Typical entry titles and core tasks
- AI substitution pressure (Low / Medium / High)
- Skills increasing and declining in hiring demand
- Current discussion themes from professional discourse
- Direct curriculum implications

This grounds the assessment in the labour market as it exists now, not as it was when the degree was designed.

### The Curriculum Redesign Advisor

The Curriculum Redesign Advisor converts assessment scores and market evidence into an actionable curriculum improvement plan with:
- Diagnostic summary of scoring weaknesses
- Score-to-action mapping for every weak dimension
- Prioritised interventions table (impact, effort, owner, timeline, KPI, market signal link)
- 12-month implementation roadmap
- 24-month capability roadmap
- Assessment redesign examples (before/after)
- AI governance and quality controls
- Measurement plan with leading and lagging indicators

## What Problem This Solves — In Plain Terms

**For a student choosing a degree:** Right now, students make a three-year, $40,000+ investment decision with almost no information about whether the qualification will hold its value. COMPASS gives students and their families an honest, evidence-based answer before they enrol.

**For a curriculum designer or academic lead:** Curriculum review is currently slow, opinion-heavy, and anecdotal. COMPASS gives curriculum teams a structured, evidence-grounded starting point for redesign conversations. It identifies exactly which parts of a program are vulnerable and why, with specific intervention options.

**For a faculty dean or program director:** Institutional leaders need to make resource allocation decisions across multiple degrees simultaneously. COMPASS makes it possible to benchmark an entire portfolio of programs against a consistent rubric and prioritise redesign investment where it is most needed.

**For an employer or industry partner:** Employers spend significant resources onboarding and retraining graduates who arrive with the wrong capability profile. COMPASS-aligned programs signal that graduates have been trained for the actual demands of AI-augmented work.

**For an accreditation or quality assurance body:** Existing QA frameworks measure inputs and process. COMPASS introduces a labour-market durability lens that makes future-employability a measurable dimension of program quality.

## Why This Is an Education Innovation Problem — Not Just a Technology Problem

COMPASS is not primarily a technology product. Generative AI is an enabler, and the structured quality-assurance framework ensures outputs are evidence-based, repeatable, and decision-ready. The core innovation is:

1. **A new evaluation framework** — a rubric that separates academic quality from labour-market durability, which does not currently exist in any standardised form in Australian higher education.

2. **A new workflow for curriculum review** — replacing slow, opinion-heavy committee processes with a fast, evidence-grounded first-pass that curriculum teams can act on.

3. **A new information channel for students** — giving prospective students access to honest, structured viability evidence at the point of course selection, not retrospectively.

4. **A new accountability mechanism for institutions** — making it possible to track whether curriculum interventions actually shift a program's labour-market durability score over time.

The technology accelerates the process. The framework defines what gets measured and why.

## Current State of Development

COMPASS has been built and validated as a working proof-of-concept. It currently:

- Accepts any university program URL, course code, or curriculum description as input.
- Fetches and analyses publicly available handbook data as evidence.
- Produces structured assessment reports across all three capabilities with a fixed, repeatable output contract.
- Generates market-intelligence scans across relevant job families.
- Produces implementation-ready curriculum improvement plans.
- Has been validated against the University of Melbourne Bachelor of Design, producing a score of 17/36 (HIGH RISK) with 6 evidence-grounded, market-linked recommendations.

The proof-of-concept demonstrates viability of the core analytical framework. The vision is a purpose-built SaaS platform accessible to any academic or professional staff member through a browser — no technical knowledge required. It does not yet have that user interface, structured data pipelines for live job-ad extraction, or integration with institutional systems such as LMS or curriculum management tools.

## What Discover Would Help Us Build

The Discover program addresses exactly the questions that must be answered before this moves from a working prototype to an impactful product.

### The questions we need to answer with Discover support:

**1. What problem are we really solving?**  
We have a strong hypothesis, but we need to validate whether the primary value is in (a) student decision support, (b) curriculum review tooling for academic staff, or (c) institutional portfolio risk management. These have different design requirements, different stakeholders, and different implementation paths.

**2. Who needs to be involved to make this work?**  
Meaningful adoption requires curriculum owners, quality assurance leads, careers and employability teams, and potentially external employers and accreditation bodies. We need to identify the right internal and external stakeholders and understand their actual workflows, constraints, and incentives.

**3. What needs to be built so people will use it and love using it?**  
The proof-of-concept validates the analytical framework. The product vision is a browser-based SaaS tool any curriculum coordinator or academic lead can use without technical support. We need to understand what interface, integration, and workflow design would make this genuinely usable by the people it is designed for — and test that with real users before committing to a build.

**4. Is there real demand, or are we solving a problem we find interesting?**  
The Discover program's concept-testing process is exactly what is needed to answer this. We need to talk to academic staff, program directors, students, and careers teams about whether this addresses a real and urgent enough pain to justify adoption.

**5. What does a responsible, trustworthy version of this look like?**  
An AI tool that produces risk ratings for university degrees has significant implications. We need to think carefully about transparency, explainability, bias, institutional trust, and how to frame outputs so they are genuinely useful rather than reductive or damaging.

**By the end of Discover:**
- Validated understanding of the primary user and their actual problem.

## Why Now

The urgency is not theoretical. The World Economic Forum's 2025 Future of Jobs report projects that 40% of current skills will need to change within five years. Generative AI capability benchmarks crossed entry-level knowledge-work thresholds in 2023–2024 and continue to improve. The first cohorts of graduates whose degrees were designed before the current AI inflection point are entering the labour market now.

Universities that do not develop a systematic way to assess and respond to this shift will discover the gap retrospectively — at graduate employment rates, at attrition, and at reputational cost. COMPASS is a tool that lets institutions find and close that gap proactively, before it becomes a crisis.

## Appendix: Sample Output — Bachelor of Design (B-DES), University of Melbourne

The following is an abbreviated example of a COMPASS assessment run against the UniMelb Bachelor of Design using the proof-of-concept:

- **Score:** 17/36
- **Risk band:** HIGH RISK

## Appendix: Full Three-Report Workflow — Master of Information Systems (MC-IS), University of Melbourne

The following demonstrates the complete COMPASS workflow: viability assessment, market intelligence scan, and curriculum improvement plan — applied to the UniMelb Master of Information Systems. All three reports were produced in a single session, illustrating the speed and evidence quality the tool delivers compared to a traditional curriculum advisory process.

**DFVA Viability Assessment** ([reports/dfva-mc-is.md](reports/dfva-mc-is.md))
- **Score:** 18/36
- **Risk band:** HIGH RISK
- **Q1** (AI can produce 80% of first-2-year output?): **YES**
- **Q2** (Trains graduates to design systems, own decisions?): **UNCERTAIN**
- **Q3** (Graduates more employable in 5 years?): **NO**
- **Critical gaps:** D1 Automation Exposure (1/3), D5 AI Literacy and Governance (1/3), D10 Graduate Outcome Evidence (1/3)
- **Verdict:** The program anchors early-career outcomes in human middleware tasks — documentation, requirements analysis, coordination, and reporting — that are the current front line of AI-driven role compression. Enroll with a clear upskilling plan, not as a standalone credential.

**Market Intelligence Scan** ([reports/dfva-market-mc-is.md](reports/dfva-market-mc-is.md))
- 8 job families profiled across years 1–5 post-graduation
- Entry-level BA, IT analyst, and reporting analyst ANZ postings down 18–22% YoY
- "Automation Analyst" postings up 47% YoY; "AI Governance Analyst" appearing as new ANZ title category
- Key skill shifts: manual documentation and JIRA administration declining fast; AI governance frameworks (ISO 42001, NIST AI RMF) and data engineering rising very fast

**Curriculum Improvement Plan** ([reports/dfva-recommend-mc-is.md](reports/dfva-recommend-mc-is.md))
- 8 prioritised interventions with owner, timeline, and measurable KPI
- 12-month implementation roadmap (foundation → design sprint → build → pre-launch)
- 24-month capability roadmap: projected score lift from 18/36 (HIGH RISK) to 28–30/36 (RESILIENT)
- Assessment redesign examples: Systems Analysis and Design, IT Project Management, Capstone (all tracks)
- Projected score at Month 12: 23–24/36 (MODERATE RISK)
- Projected score at Month 24: 28–30/36 (RESILIENT)
