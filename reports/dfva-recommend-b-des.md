## 1. DIAGNOSTIC SUMMARY

The Bachelor of Design scored **17/36 — HIGH RISK**. Three critical 1/3 scores define the primary intervention targets.

| Dimension | Score | Status |
|---|---|---|
| D1 — Automation Exposure of Roles | 2/3 | Adequate |
| D2 — Systems Thinking and Problem Framing | 2/3 | Adequate |
| D3 — Technical and Quantitative Depth | 1/3 | ⚠️ Critical gap |
| D4 — Decision-Making Under Uncertainty | 2/3 | Adequate |
| D5 — AI Literacy and Governance | 1/3 | ⚠️ Critical gap |
| D6 — Domain Depth and Specialization | 2/3 | Adequate |
| D7 — Research Methods Rigor | 2/3 | Adequate |
| D8 — Human and Relational Capability | 3/3 | ✅ Strong |
| D9 — Curriculum Currency and Adaptability | 2/3 | Adequate |
| D10 — Graduate Outcome Evidence | 1/3 | ⚠️ Critical gap |
| B — Irreplaceability Premium (Bonus) | 2/3 | Adequate |
| **TOTAL** | **21/36** | **MODERATE RISK** |

The B-Des has a genuine human-relational strength (D8: 3/3). Studio culture, critique practice, and embodied making are not easily replicated by AI. The problem is structural: these strengths are undermined by three 1/3 scores that represent the degree's primary intervention targets.

**D3 — Technical Depth:** Design graduates without prototyping, code literacy, or data fluency cannot govern or direct the AI tools now embedded in every design workflow. Tool consumption without technical depth is the most exposed graduate profile in 2027.

**D5 — AI Literacy and Governance:** No mandatory AI governance unit. Graduates will enter a profession where Figma AI, Midjourney, and agentic design tools are baseline — without the critical evaluation framework to distinguish good AI-augmented design from bad.

**D10 — Graduate Outcome Evidence:** Without granular destination data, the degree cannot demonstrate its value proposition to prospective students, employers, or institutional funders. This is both a quality assurance failure and a competitive disadvantage.

## 7. ASSESSMENT REDESIGN EXAMPLES

**Example A — Capstone Studio (Redesigned)**

Current (inferred): Complete a major design project responding to a studio brief. Produce a resolved design outcome with supporting documentation and present to academic panel.

Redesigned: Complete a major design project for a live industry, government, or community partner. Required components: (1) a co-designed brief with the client, documented constraints and success criteria; (2) a research phase using both qualitative and quantitative methods — at least one usability test with statistical analysis or one survey with design implications; (3) a design decision log documenting all major trade-offs, what was rejected and why; (4) an AI tool use reflection — if AI tools were used at any stage, document which tools, how outputs were evaluated, what required human judgment, and what the tool got wrong; (5) a live presentation to client stakeholders with challenge questions; (6) a client satisfaction statement. Assessment criteria: research rigour, decision accountability, AI governance, stakeholder communication, and design resolution. Dimensions: D4, D5, D7, D8, B.

**Example B — AI in Design Practice (New Unit Assessment)**

Assessment 1 (40%) — AI Tool Evaluation: Select an AI design tool relevant to your specialisation (e.g. Figma AI, Midjourney, Adobe Firefly, Galileo AI). Produce a structured evaluation: (1) what the tool does and what design tasks it claims to replace or augment; (2) training data basis and what biases or gaps that creates for design outputs; (3) three specific failure modes — situations where the tool produces outputs that a competent designer would reject and why; (4) an IP and copyright risk assessment for commercial use; (5) a governance recommendation: under what conditions should this tool be used, with what human oversight, and with what client disclosure.

Assessment 2 (60%) — AI Workflow Direction: You are the lead designer on a project where the client wants to use AI tools to accelerate delivery. Produce a design direction brief: (1) an AI workflow map showing which tasks are AI-assisted, which are human-led, and why; (2) a quality assurance protocol for AI-generated design outputs; (3) an IP and ethics disclosure for the client; (4) a design rationale explaining what AI cannot replace in this project and why; (5) present to a panel including at least one non-designer stakeholder. Dimensions: D5, D2, D4, D1.

**Example C — Design and Technology Integration (Assessed Prototype)**

Current: No mandatory technical unit.

New assessment: Produce a functional interactive prototype using: (1) Figma or Framer for interaction design; (2) basic HTML/CSS implementation demonstrating understanding of how design translates to build constraints; (3) a data visualisation component using real data relevant to your design context; (4) an accessibility audit against WCAG 2.2 AA with documented remediation decisions. Assessment criteria: prototype fidelity, build-constraint awareness, data integration, accessibility compliance. No prior coding experience assumed — the unit teaches what designers need to know to direct technical implementation, not to replace developers. Dimensions: D3, D6, B.

## 8. AI GOVERNANCE AND QUALITY CONTROLS

**AI tool disclosure policy:** All studio units adopt a consistent AI disclosure policy by Sem 1 2027. Students declare AI tool use in a reflection log for all major assessments. This is a professional practice habit, not a prohibition.

**AI in Design Practice currency:** The unit coordinator must review and update the AI tool landscape each semester. Design AI is moving faster than annual curriculum review cycles. Build in a semester-by-semester tool review as a unit management requirement.

**Live-client AI disclosure:** Any capstone project using AI tools must include a client-facing AI disclosure statement. This normalises professional-grade AI governance from graduation.

**Portfolio authenticity controls:** All portfolio artifacts require a process documentation component — not just final outputs. AI-generated outputs without documented human direction and critique do not meet portfolio standards. Capstone client statement confirms student authorship and AI disclosure.

**COMPASS assessment controls:** Re-run COMPASS assessment annually after each advisory panel review. Score changes of ±3 in any dimension trigger curriculum review within 60 days.

## 10. RISKS, TRADE-OFFS, AND DEPENDENCIES

| Risk | Likelihood | Impact | Mitigation |
|---|---|---|---|
| Academic governance delays technical unit beyond 2028 | MEDIUM | HIGH | Begin consultation Month 1; frame as design professional literacy, not coding requirement; pilot as elective in 2027 while mandate is in approval |
| AI in Design Practice unit resisted as "not design" by studio faculty | MEDIUM | HIGH | Frame explicitly as design governance and direction; invite industry co-designers with design backgrounds; use design critique methodology in assessment structure |
| Live-client capstone partner pipeline insufficient for mandatory requirement | MEDIUM | HIGH | Begin partner audit Month 3; expand to government, NFP, and social enterprise; allow research-group embedded projects as alternative |
| Specialisation destination data requires IT system investment beyond Faculty control | MEDIUM | MEDIUM | Partner with Institutional Research early; frame as student recruitment value proposition |
| Technical unit adds assessment load disadvantaging part-time or international students | LOW–MEDIUM | MEDIUM | Design unit around design direction, not coding fluency; no prior technical experience assumed; provide additional tutorial support |

**Key trade-offs:**

Studio culture vs. technical mandate: Making a technical foundations unit mandatory changes the character of the degree. Some studio faculty will resist. The trade-off is necessary — a design degree that produces graduates unable to prototype at industry pace or evaluate AI tool outputs is not preparing them for 2027. Frame it as expanding the definition of design craft, not replacing it.

Speed vs. governance: Both new mandatory units require Academic Board approval — 12–18 month minimum. Use the interim period to pilot both units as electives, redesign existing unit assessments to include AI governance components, and build industry partner relationships before the mandate takes effect.

**Dependencies:** MSD advisory panel must be constituted before AI in Design Practice learning outcomes can be externally validated. Specialisation destination data requires Careers and Employability Office and Institutional Research partnership. Live-client capstone depends on industry partner network capacity — stress-test before mandate.

## 11. REDESIGNED GRADUATE PROFILE (2027 READY)

The 2027-ready B-Des graduate understood that directing an AI design tool without technical literacy is like directing a film without understanding the camera. They understood that in 2027, the distinction between designer and technologist had collapsed — and that the graduates who thrived were the ones who embraced the expanded craft, not the ones who defended the old boundaries.

They completed AI in Design Practice in second year and came out understanding that Figma AI generates components but cannot make the judgment call about whether the interaction pattern serves the user. They know what Midjourney's training data biases are, why that matters for representation in brand work, and what a client disclosure statement needs to say. They have written a governance brief for an AI tool and defended it to a panel that included a lawyer and a non-designer client.

They built a functional interactive prototype — not because they became a developer, but because they know exactly what constraints the build team is working within, and they can prototype fast enough to test an idea before anyone writes a line of production code.

Their capstone was a live project with a real government or industry client. They presented to stakeholders who asked hard questions they did not know the answer to in advance. Their design decision log shows every trade-off they made and why. Their client satisfaction statement is in their portfolio.

They are the graduate that a design strategy team, a service design consultancy, or an AI product company hires because they can do something that AI cannot: sit in a room with a human being, understand what that person actually needs, make a judgment call about what matters, and then direct the tools — AI and human — to get there.
