## 1. DIAGNOSTIC SUMMARY

The B-Sci scored **23/36 — MODERATE RISK**. The score distribution reveals a degree with genuine technical and research strengths that are unevenly distributed across its graduate population and undermined by two critical gaps in AI literacy and human/relational capability.

### Primary Failure Pattern

The B-Sci scores 3/3 on three dimensions — technical depth, domain specialization, and research methods rigor. These are real and defensible strengths. The problem is structural: these strengths are **only fully realized for graduates who complete deep majors, undertake a research project, and proceed to honours or postgraduate study**. Three-year generalist graduates — particularly from life sciences majors — exit with a credential that signals broad scientific literacy without the specialist depth that creates automation resistance.

The two 1/3 scores define the primary intervention targets:

- **D5 — AI Literacy and Governance:** A science degree in 2026 with no mandatory AI governance unit is a curriculum currency failure. Every scientific domain now deploys AI tooling; graduates cannot govern what they were never trained to question.
- **D8 — Human and Relational Capability:** As AI absorbs the technical execution layer of scientific work, interpersonal judgment, ethical reasoning, and stakeholder communication become the primary human value-add. The degree does not assess this systematically.

## 7. ASSESSMENT REDESIGN EXAMPLES

### Example A: Research Project — Redesigned

**Redesigned requirement:**
Complete a supervised primary research project. Required components: (1) a pre-registration document stating hypothesis, methodology, and predicted limitations before data collection begins; (2) a primary data collection and analysis phase with documented methodology; (3) a failure-mode and limitation analysis — what did not work, why, and what it means for interpretation; (4) an AI tool use reflection — if AI tools were used at any stage, document which tools, how outputs were validated, what the model's limitations are in this research context, and what would have been missed without human judgment; (5) a 10-minute panel defence of methodology with examiner challenge.

**Dimensions targeted:** D4, D5, D7, B

### Example B: AI in Scientific Research — New Unit Assessment

**Assessment 1 (40%):** Select an AI tool currently used in your major's research domain (e.g., AlphaFold for biochemistry, climate ML models for earth science). Produce a structured evaluation: (1) what the tool does; (2) what data it was trained on and what biases that creates; (3) three failure modes where the tool would produce misleading outputs; (4) what a scientist would need to do to validate the tool's output before publishing; (5) who is accountable when the tool is wrong.

**Assessment 2 (60%):** You are advising a research group that wants to use an AI tool to accelerate a phase of their research. Produce a governance brief: (1) tool assessment using NIST AI RMF or equivalent; (2) research integrity checklist; (3) disclosure and reproducibility requirements for publication; (4) recommendation with a justified go/no-go decision. Present to a panel including at least one researcher unfamiliar with the tool.

**Dimensions targeted:** D5, D2, D4

### Example C: Science Communication and Ethics — Redesigned Assessment

**Final assessment:** You are a scientist advising a government department, community organisation, or media outlet on a topic within your major. The audience has no specialist background. Produce: (1) a stakeholder brief (800 words maximum) translating your research area's current state of evidence on a defined question; (2) an AI disclosure statement — if you used AI tools to assist in drafting, what did you use, how did you verify the output, and what required your judgment; (3) a live Q&A session (15 minutes) with a panel including at least one non-scientist.

**Dimensions targeted:** D8, D5, D4

## 8. AI GOVERNANCE AND QUALITY CONTROLS

- **Tool disclosure:** Every unit in the degree should adopt a consistent AI tool disclosure policy by Sem 1 2027. Students declare AI tool use in a reflection log for all major assessments.
- **Research Project AI reflection:** The AI use reflection must be mandatory and assessable — assign 10% of the project grade to AI governance reflection quality.
- **Assessment integrity:** AI in Scientific Research assessments must use current, real AI tools in the student's specific domain. Update tool examples each semester as the landscape changes.
- **Curriculum review trigger:** If a major AI governance framework (NIST AI RMF, ISO 42001, ARC Research Integrity Guidelines) updates materially, the unit coordinator must review and update content within one semester.
- **Pre-registration requirement:** In the redesigned Research Project closes a structural reproducibility gap and cannot be waived.
- **AI-generated data validation:** Any research project using AI-generated or AI-assisted data must include a documented human validation step.
- **Portfolio authenticity:** The graduation portfolio must include a supervisor or client confirmation statement for each artifact.
- **COMPASS re-assessment:** Re-run annually after each curriculum advisory panel review. Score changes of ±3 or more in any dimension trigger a curriculum review meeting within 60 days.

## 10. RISKS, TRADE-OFFS, AND DEPENDENCIES

| Risk | Likelihood | Impact | Mitigation |
|---|---|---|---|
| Academic governance delays Research Project mandate beyond 2028 | HIGH | HIGH | Begin consultation in Month 1; frame as labor-market risk not administrative change; pilot opt-in expansion in 2027 while mandate is in approval |
| AI in Scientific Research unit resisted by non-computational major coordinators as "not our domain" | MEDIUM | HIGH | Frame explicitly as research governance, not computing; co-design with School of Historical and Philosophical Studies; include domain-specific AI examples for every major |
| Science Internship employer partner pipeline insufficient for mandatory requirement | MEDIUM | HIGH | Begin partner audit Month 3; expand to government agencies, NFPs, and research institutes; allow research-group embedded internship as alternative |
| Science Communication and Ethics unit lacks qualified teaching staff | MEDIUM | MEDIUM | Use practitioner adjuncts from science journalism, APS science policy, and science communication industry; co-teach with Faculty of Arts |
| Major-level destination data requires IT system investment beyond Faculty control | MEDIUM | MEDIUM | Partner with Institutional Research early; frame as student recruitment value proposition |
| Graduation portfolio adds assessment load disadvantaging part-time or working students | LOW–MEDIUM | MEDIUM | Design portfolio as integration of existing required artifacts, not additional assessment |

**Key trade-offs:** Making the Research Project and Science Internship mandatory reduces elective flexibility — this trade-off is necessary. Speed vs. governance: the Research Project mandate requires Academic Board approval (12–18 months minimum); use the interim to redesign assessment format within the existing optional structure. Universal AI unit vs. major-specific AI content: core unit covers governance and evaluation methodology; major-specific AI content added within each major's existing units.

**Dependencies:** Curriculum advisory panel must be constituted before major-level advising materials can be externally validated. Major-level destination data requires Careers and Employability Office and Institutional Research partnership. Science Internship mandate depends on employer partner network capacity — must be stress-tested before mandate takes effect. Research Project mandate requires individual major coordinators to confirm supervisory capacity.

## 11. REDESIGNED GRADUATE PROFILE (2027 READY)

The 2027-ready B-Sci graduate did not coast through a broad science degree hoping that scientific literacy would be enough. They chose a major with genuine technical depth, completed a primary research project with pre-registration and a defended methodology, and finished their degree able to look at an AI tool in their domain and tell you exactly where it fails.

They took the AI in Scientific Research unit in second year and came out understanding that AlphaFold is not a substitute for experimental validation, that climate models have training data boundaries that matter for regulatory decisions, and that every AI-assisted research output needs a documented human accountability step before it can be published or acted on. They know this not because they were warned about AI — but because they were trained to evaluate it.

They completed a Science Internship in a regulated or high-stakes environment: a biotech firm, a government science agency, or an environmental consultancy. They have a client confirmation statement and a documented decision-making reflection proving they operated under real uncertainty with real consequences.

Their graduation portfolio has three artifacts that no AI can produce for them: a primary research dataset with a defended methodology, an AI governance brief for a tool in their domain, and a stakeholder communication piece reviewed by a non-specialist panel.

They are not afraid of AI in their field. They are the person their research group or employer relies on to decide which AI outputs to trust — and why.
