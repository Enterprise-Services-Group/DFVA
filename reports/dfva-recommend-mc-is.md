## 7. ASSESSMENT REDESIGN EXAMPLES

### Example A: Systems Analysis and Design

**Current (inferred):**
> Produce a Business Requirements Document for a given case study. Demonstrate requirements elicitation, stakeholder analysis, and process documentation.

**Redesigned:**
> Conduct an automation audit of three nominated business processes. For each: (1) identify AI substitution candidates with justification; (2) specify the human-in-the-loop boundary; (3) design a governance framework including failure-mode analysis and escalation protocols; (4) produce a change readiness brief for the executive sponsor. Defend recommendations in a 15-minute panel review.

**Dimensions targeted:** D1, D2, D4

---

### Example B: IT Project Management

**Current (inferred):**
> Develop a project plan including risk register, resource schedule, communication plan, and status reporting framework.

**Redesigned:**
> Your team is implementing an AI workflow automation system for a client in your sector cluster. Produce: (1) a risk-weighted implementation plan identifying where AI tooling is used in delivery itself; (2) a stakeholder change management plan addressing AI adoption resistance; (3) a governance protocol for the deployed system post-implementation. Present to a simulated client board with one dissenting stakeholder role-played by the assessor.

**Dimensions targeted:** D4, D5, D8

---

### Example C: Capstone

**Current (inferred):**
> Research project on an IS topic of your choice. Produce a thesis or extended research paper.

**Redesigned (all tracks):**
> Working with a confirmed industry client, design and document an AI workflow implementation. Deliverables: (1) current-state automation audit; (2) proposed AI workflow design with human-in-the-loop specification; (3) governance and compliance framework (NIST AI RMF, ISO 42001, applicable sector regulation); (4) change management plan; (5) failure-mode and risk register; (6) post-implementation measurement plan. Present to client and academic panel. Client must confirm the project addressed a real organizational problem.

**Dimensions targeted:** D1, D4, D5, D6, B

## 8. AI GOVERNANCE AND QUALITY CONTROLS

- **Assessment transparency:** Every AI-related assessment must specify which tools students may use, under what constraints, and with what disclosure. Students document AI tool use in a reflection log.
- **Curriculum review trigger:** If ISO 42001 or NIST AI RMF update materially, unit coordinator must review content within one semester — not at the next triennial review.
- **External validation:** Unit learning outcomes mapped against ISO 42001, NIST AI RMF, or ACS AI Ethics framework; reviewed by employer advisory panel annually.
- **Capstone integrity:** Must include live client confirmation letter that the project addressed a real problem. No simulated client substitution.
- **COMPASS re-assessment:** Run annually after employer advisory panel review. Score changes of ±3 in any dimension trigger curriculum review within 60 days.

**Key trade-offs:**
- Adding mandatory AI Governance unit and sector clusters displaces elective flexibility — a necessary trade-off
- Mandating real-client capstone removes theoretical thesis option from coursework stream — MC-IS is a professional degree and should be assessed as one
- Use interim period to redesign assessments within approved unit shells before full academic board approval to avoid velocity loss

**Dependencies:**
- Employer Advisory Panel must be constituted before sector cluster design can be validated
- Graduate destination tracking requires Careers team partnership
- Real-client capstone requires signed partnership agreements (allow time for legal review)

## 11. REDESIGNED GRADUATE PROFILE (2027 READY)

The 2027-ready MC-IS graduate arrives in an organisation knowing that the job title on their offer letter is probably not the role they'll be doing within 18 months — and they are ready for that. They have spent two years learning to identify which workflows in an organisation are candidates for AI substitution, where the human-in-the-loop boundary must hold, and how to design the governance framework that makes that boundary enforceable and auditable.

They have a sector. Not "IT in general" — they know health informatics regulation, or financial systems compliance, or government data governance. That sector knowledge means they can walk into a regulated environment and immediately understand why the AI tool the vendor is selling cannot be deployed the way the vendor claims.

They can build a basic data pipeline: not at a data engineer's depth, but enough to evaluate whether the data feeding an AI system is trustworthy — and to brief the engineering team with precision when it is not.

They have a capstone artifact: a documented AI workflow implementation for a real client, including failure modes, the governance framework, and the change management plan. That is a portfolio piece that a generic BA graduate, an IT coordinator, or a fresh MBA cannot produce.

They do not *use* AI tools. They **govern, supervise, and redesign** them. That is the role that will exist in 2029.
