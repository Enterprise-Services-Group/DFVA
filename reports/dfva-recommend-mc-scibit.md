## 7. ASSESSMENT REDESIGN EXAMPLES

**Example A — Industry Project (AI Reflection Addition)**

Current: Industry Project with external company — group work, year-long, real brief.

Addition (mandatory new component of final report): (1) AI tools inventory — list all AI tools used by the industry partner and by the student group; (2) for each tool: tasks it was used for, how outputs were validated, what required human judgment, what would have happened if the output had been wrong; (3) a governance recommendation — what the company should do about AI governance for this tool, with reference to TGA, FDA, NHMRC, or industry-specific regulatory framework; (4) a reflection on how AI changed the nature of the project work and what the students' human value-add was. Dimensions: D4, D5, B.

**Example B — AI in Biotechnology (New Unit)**

Assessment 1 (40%) — Regulated AI Tool Evaluation: Select an AI tool relevant to the biotechnology sector (e.g. AlphaFold 3, Benchling AI, an AI regulatory submission tool, a clinical trial AI platform). Produce a structured evaluation: what the tool does; training data basis and reliability implications for your regulatory context; three specific failure modes; what TGA or FDA guidance says about AI tool use in this context; a governance recommendation covering validation requirements, human oversight, and regulatory disclosure.

Assessment 2 (60%) — AI Governance Advisory: You are a regulatory affairs or QA professional at a biotechnology company. Management wants to use an AI tool to assist with TGA submissions or GMP documentation. Produce a governance brief: AI tool assessment using the TGA AI framework; validation protocol; regulatory disclosure requirements; go/no-go recommendation with justification; present to a panel including a non-scientist stakeholder. Dimensions: D5, D6, D2, D4.

**Example C — MAST90072 Data and Decision Making (AI Validation Module)**

Add module: Students receive a dataset and an AI-generated analysis. Task: (1) reproduce the analysis independently; (2) identify what the AI got right, what it got wrong, and what it missed; (3) produce a corrected analysis with documented validation steps; (4) write a one-page governance note for using AI-assisted data analysis in a regulated biotech context. Dimensions: D3, D5, D7.

## 8. AI GOVERNANCE AND QUALITY CONTROLS

**AI in Biotechnology unit currency:** TGA and FDA AI guidance is changing quarterly. The unit coordinator must review and update AI regulatory framework content each semester. Build in bi-annual content review aligned with the industry advisory panel review cycle.

**Industry Project AI disclosure policy:** All Industry Project groups adopt a consistent AI disclosure policy aligned with GCP principles — any AI tool used is documented, validated, and disclosed. This is a professional practice requirement, not a restriction.

**Regulatory context specificity:** AI governance content must be sector-specific — TGA AI guidance for medical devices and therapeutics, FDA AI action plan, ICH guidelines on AI, NHMRC AI research integrity framework. Generic AI ethics content is insufficient for regulated biotech contexts.

**COMPASS re-assessment:** Re-run COMPASS annually after each industry advisory panel review. Score changes of ±3 in any dimension trigger curriculum review within 60 days.

## 10. RISKS, TRADE-OFFS, AND DEPENDENCIES

| Risk | Likelihood | Impact | Mitigation |
|---|---|---|---|
| AI in Biotechnology unit resisted as "too regulatory" by science faculty | MEDIUM | HIGH | Frame as professional science master's core competency; co-design with Melbourne Law School and industry partners; use TGA and FDA guidance documents as primary curriculum anchors |
| SCIE90005 and LAWS90129 remain unavailable beyond 2026 | MEDIUM | MEDIUM | Already mitigated by integrating AI content into BTCH90010 and LAWS90003 — do not wait for unit restoration |
| Industry advisory panel difficult to form given industry partner time constraints | LOW–MEDIUM | MEDIUM | Leverage existing AusBiotech relationships; offer advisory panel members guest lecture invitations and early access to destination data |
| Computational elective mandate increases withdrawal risk for non-quantitative students | LOW | MEDIUM | Ensure BIOL90041 Data Science for Biologists is available as accessible entry point; provide bridging support |
| Program destination data requires IT investment beyond Faculty control | MEDIUM | MEDIUM | Partner with Institutional Research early; frame as competitive advantage vs. Monash and RMIT equivalents |

**Key trade-off:** The degree is already 200 credit points. Adding a mandatory AI unit competes with elective choice. The least disruptive path is to mandate AI in Biotechnology within the 75cp elective allocation. Alternatively, integrating AI content into BTCH90010, LAWS90003, and MAST90072 achieves similar coverage without load increase — this is the recommended interim path while the full unit is in development.

**Dependencies:** Industry advisory panel must be constituted before AI in Biotechnology learning outcomes can be validated against current TGA/FDA guidance. Program destination data requires Careers and Employability and Institutional Research partnership. Computational elective mandate requires curriculum committee approval — begin Month 1.

## 11. REDESIGNED GRADUATE PROFILE (2027 READY)

The 2027-ready MC-SCIBIT graduate chose this degree because they understood that biotechnology in 2027 is not primarily a science degree — it is a decision-making degree. The science is increasingly AI-assisted. The human value is in deciding whether the AI got it right, and taking legal and professional responsibility for that decision.

They completed AI in Biotechnology and can tell you what TGA requires before an AI-generated regulatory submission is accepted, what AlphaFold's failure modes are in novel protein prediction contexts, and what NIST AI RMF requires of a company deploying AI in a GMP environment. Their Industry Project was with a company using AI tools — and their final report included a governance brief for that tool that the company's QA director actually used.

They completed Genomics and Bioinformatics as their mandatory computational elective and can work with sequence data, understand what an AI genomics tool is doing, and evaluate whether its output requires experimental validation. They are not a bioinformatician — but they can direct one and they can evaluate the output.

They are the regulatory affairs professional, the technology transfer officer, and the AI governance analyst that an ANZ biotech company hires because they can sit across the table from a TGA assessor and say: we used AI to draft this submission, here is the validation protocol, here is why it is accurate — and I am signing it.
