**Assessment Date:** 2026-05-15 | **Source URL:** https://handbook.unimelb.edu.au/2025/courses/mc-sciear | **Prompt Version:** DFVA-COPILOT-SKILL-v1

## 1. DIAGNOSTIC SUMMARY

The Master of Science (Earth Sciences) scores 24/36 (MODERATE RISK). Its fundamental architecture is strong: a 125-point research project creates genuine automation resistance, deep domain specialization in both streams provides durable expertise, and physical fieldwork requirements make graduates structurally difficult to replace with AI. The program's weaknesses are concentrated in two dimensions: AI Literacy (1/3) and Human & Relational Capability (1/3), with Outcome Evidence (1/3) as a third gap. These are addressable without restructuring the program's core research architecture.

**Key gaps by dimension:**
- D5 (AI Literacy): No mandatory AI governance unit. LAWS90203 not available. Graduates enter a discipline increasingly using AI tools (automated logging, ML-assisted modelling) without training in tool evaluation or responsible deployment.
- D8 (Human & Relational): Communication subjects optional. Limited collaborative or stakeholder-facing requirements beyond thesis oral presentations.
- D10 (Outcome Evidence): No published graduate destination data by stream or role type.
- D9 (Curriculum Currency): Several subjects "not available" or "no longer available" suggesting maintenance gaps.

## 7. ASSESSMENT REDESIGN EXAMPLES

**Example A: AI Methodology Chapter in Research Thesis — New Requirement.** Within the existing 125pt research project, add a mandatory 2,000-word chapter: "AI Tools, Limitations, and Validation in [Research Topic]." Assessment criteria (10% of literature review component): identify AI tools relevant to the research domain; evaluate training data and known failure modes; describe validation approach used; reflect on when AI outputs were accepted vs rejected and why. Dimensions: D5, D4, D7.

**Example B: Critical Evaluation of AI-Generated Geological Interpretation — New Assessment.** Within an existing Geology stream core subject (e.g., GEOL90048 Sedimentary Basins and Resource Analysis): provide students with AI-generated basin interpretation alongside raw data. Assessment (20% of subject): identify errors, biases, and limitations in the AI output; produce a corrected interpretation with justification; write a 1,000-word governance brief on deployment risks. Dimensions: D5, D6, B.

**Example C: Communication for Research Scientists — Enhanced Assessment.** Within the now-compulsory SCIE90013: add a "translate your research for non-specialist decision-makers" component. Students present their research findings to a panel including industry practitioners (not just academics). Assessment includes stakeholder question handling and uncertainty communication. Dimensions: D8, D4.

## 8. AI GOVERNANCE AND QUALITY CONTROLS

**Research integrity:** The AI methodology chapter creates an auditable record of AI tool use in student research. This addresses emerging concerns about undisclosed AI use in theses while training students in responsible practice.

**Assessment integrity:** AI tool evaluation assessments are inherently resistant to AI gaming — they require students to identify when AI is wrong, which requires genuine domain expertise that AI cannot self-assess.

**Professional standards pathway:** AI governance training aligns with emerging professional standards in geoscience (AusIMM, AIG, AMOS) that increasingly reference responsible AI deployment in geological and environmental reporting.

**Quality control cycle:** Advisory panel reviews AI curriculum content annually against industry practice changes. Student feedback on AI module relevance collected each semester. Graduate destination data used to validate that AI-literate graduates achieve stronger placement outcomes.

## 11. REDESIGNED GRADUATE PROFILE (2027 READY)

The 2027 MC-SCIEAR graduate is a field-capable earth scientist who commands both physical and computational domains. In geology, they conduct fieldwork (mapping, drilling, sampling), interpret geological systems, and critically evaluate AI-generated outputs (automated logs, ML-based resource estimates, satellite-derived geological maps) — knowing exactly when to trust and when to challenge automated interpretations. In atmospheric science, they validate climate models against observations, diagnose model failures, and communicate forecast uncertainty to government and industry decision-makers.

Both streams produce graduates who can articulate the limitations of AI tools in their domain, document responsible AI use in their research, and communicate complex scientific uncertainty to non-specialist audiences. Their 25,000-word thesis includes an AI methodology chapter demonstrating critical evaluation of tool use — a marker of scientific integrity that employers and PhD supervisors increasingly expect.

Their irreplaceability derives from a triple combination that AI cannot replicate: physical fieldwork expertise (embodied, location-specific), quantitative modelling and computation (AI-assisted but human-directed), and critical judgment about when automated outputs are wrong (requiring deep domain knowledge that comes only from primary research experience). This makes them supervisors of AI tools, not competitors with them.
