## 1. DIAGNOSTIC SUMMARY

The Master of Science (Epidemiology) scores 23/36 (MODERATE RISK). The program has exceptional quantitative depth (3/3) and research methods rigor (3/3) — the core discipline subjects constitute one of the strongest methods curricula in the Faculty. Domain depth (3/3) is also strong. The gaps are concentrated in: AI Literacy (1/3), Outcome Evidence (1/3), and moderate weaknesses in Automation Exposure (2/3) and Decision-Making Under Uncertainty (2/3). The D4 score is limited by the option to take only a 25pt minor research project (12.5% of degree), which reduces the guaranteed live-stakes decision accountability.

**Key strengths to protect:**
- Quantitative core: Biostatistics + Regression + Survival Analysis is nationally distinctive
- Epidemiological study design as inherent research methods training
- Growing sector demand (pandemic preparedness, RWE, climate health, AI governance)

**Key gaps to address:**
- D5 (AI Literacy): No AI governance content despite AI transforming epidemiological practice
- D10 (Outcome Evidence): No published graduate destination data
- D4 (Decision-Making): Minor project option (25pts) allows graduates to complete with limited research accountability

## 7. ASSESSMENT REDESIGN EXAMPLES

**Example A: AI Validation in Epidemiology — New Module.** Assessment 1 (40%): Select an AI health tool deployed in clinical or public health practice. Apply epidemiological validation framework: assess sensitivity/specificity claims, evaluate external validity across population subgroups, identify potential biases in training data, and write a 2,000-word validation protocol. Assessment 2 (60%): Design a validation study for an AI surveillance tool. Produce study protocol including: target population, reference standard, sample size justification, subgroup analysis plan, and bias mitigation strategy. Dimensions: D5, D7, B.

**Example B: AI-Augmented Biostatistics Comparison — Enhanced Assessment.** Within POPH90013 Biostatistics: provide students with a health dataset and an AI-generated statistical analysis report. Assessment (15% of subject): identify statistical errors in the AI output, assess whether assumptions are met, produce a corrected analysis with justification, and write a 500-word reflection on when AI statistical tools can be trusted. Dimensions: D5, D3.

**Example C: Epidemiology in Practice with Government Partnership — Enhanced Capstone.** Enhance POPH90243 Epidemiology in Practice with genuine government health department collaboration. Students work on real surveillance data provided by Victorian DHHS (de-identified). Assessment includes: surveillance analysis, policy brief with uncertainty communication, and oral presentation to health department panel. Dimensions: D4, D8, D2.

## 8. AI GOVERNANCE AND QUALITY CONTROLS

**Strategic positioning:** Epidemiological methods (bias assessment, validity evaluation, confounding control, population subgroup analysis) are precisely what AI health tools need for governance. This program has a unique opportunity to position its graduates as the natural AI auditors in healthcare — the people who validate whether AI tools work fairly and accurately.

**Assessment integrity:** AI validation assessments require domain expertise (understanding what constitutes valid epidemiological evidence) that AI cannot self-assess. This makes the assessments inherently resistant to AI gaming.

**Research ethics alignment:** AI validation methodology extends naturally from existing health research ethics training — both involve protecting populations from harm through rigorous methodology.

**Professional standards:** Aligns with emerging WHO, OECD, and TGA frameworks for AI health tool regulation that require epidemiological evidence of validity and safety.

## 11. REDESIGNED GRADUATE PROFILE (2027 READY)

The 2027 MC-SCIEPI graduate is an epidemiological scientist who designs studies, interprets causal evidence, and validates AI health tools using rigorous population health methodology. They combine deep quantitative expertise (biostatistics, regression, survival analysis, causal inference) with the emerging capability to audit AI diagnostic and surveillance systems for validity, fairness, and population generalizability.

Their major research project demonstrates they can frame original research questions, design observational studies that control for confounding, and generate publishable insights from complex health data. Their AI Validation training means they can evaluate whether an ML-based diagnostic tool performs equitably across population subgroups — applying epidemiological methods (sensitivity, specificity, predictive values, calibration) to a new class of health intervention.

This graduate enters a labor market with structural demand across multiple growth sectors: government pandemic preparedness (Australian CDC, state health departments), pharmaceutical real-world evidence (post-market surveillance, pharmacoepidemiology), clinical trial design (biostatistician shortage), and the emerging field of health AI governance. Their combination of methodological rigor, domain expertise, and AI validation capability positions them not as data processors who compete with AI, but as the professionals who determine whether AI health tools are safe, valid, and fair.
